# Trim

An editing pass that removes prose that doesn't earn its words. Read the target text, identify every instance of the patterns below, and rewrite to eliminate them. Preserve the author's voice and meaning — cut fat, not muscle.

## Patterns to Kill

### 1. Restating the obvious
Saying the same thing twice in different words, or explaining what the reader already understood.

**Before:** "I run Ghostty full screen on a large monitor. That's the whole setup — no tiling window manager, no elaborate workspace configuration, just one terminal emulator eating the whole screen."
**After:** "I run Ghostty full screen on a large monitor."

### 2. Over-specifying what something isn't
Listing things that don't apply instead of saying what does.

**Before:** "no tiling window manager, no elaborate workspace configuration, just one terminal emulator"
**After:** Cut entirely — the positive statement already implies this.

### 3. Throat-clearing before the point
Preamble sentences that announce what you're about to say instead of saying it.

**Before:** "The thing that makes this work is splits. Ghostty has first-class split support."
**After:** "Ghostty has first-class split support."

### 4. Hyperbolic framing
Overselling a small thing, then often walking it back.

**Before:** "The shortcut that changed everything: `Cmd+Shift+Enter`."
**After:** "A great shortcut: `Cmd+Shift+Enter`."

### 5. Performative self-awareness
Narrating your own writing process or preemptively acknowledging how something "sounds."

**Before:** "It sounds like a cosmetic preference — and it is — but it's also genuinely useful."
**After:** "It's cosmetic, but it's also genuinely useful."

### 6. Narrative permission nobody asked for
Granting yourself or the reader permission to do the thing you already described.

**Before:** "Splits appear, serve a purpose for five minutes, and disappear. That's fine."
**After:** "Splits appear, serve a purpose for five minutes, and disappear."

### 7. Two paragraphs where three sentences would do
If the information in a section can be said in fewer sentences without losing meaning, compress it.

### 8. Justifying self-evident choices
Explaining *why* you did something obvious. Trust the reader.

**Before:** "Claude Code is a CLI tool, so the terminal is where you spend your time, and once you accept that, going full screen just makes sense."
**After:** Cut — the reader knows CLI tools run in terminals.

## How to Apply

1. Read the full text first.
2. Flag every instance of the patterns above.
3. For each flag: can the sentence be cut entirely, or does it carry information that needs to survive in fewer words?
4. Rewrite. Aim for the shortest version that preserves all *information* (not all *words*).
5. Read the result aloud in your head. If it sounds choppy, restore just enough connective tissue — but no more.

## What to Preserve

- Voice and personality (this is editing, not flattening)
- Concessive turns that add real nuance ("but," "that said") — only cut these when the concession is vacuous
- Short punchy sentences used for genuine emphasis
- Concrete details, examples, and specifics — these are the opposite of filler
